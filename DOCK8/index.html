DOCK8 & Alzheimer's Disease

Target Enabling Package — AI-Assisted Evidence Summary

1. Overview

Safety Flag: Human DOCK8 loss-of-function causes severe combined immunodeficiency with recurrent infections, elevated IgE, autoimmunity, and malignancy risk. Systemic DOCK8 inhibition for AD is likely unsafe. Any therapeutic strategy must be brain- or microglia-targeted and selective.

DOCK8 encodes Dedicator of Cytokinesis 8, a guanine nucleotide exchange factor and cytoskeletal regulator operating primarily through Cdc42/Rac-related pathways. It controls immune-cell migration, actin remodeling, immune synapse formation, T- and B-cell activation, NK-cell cytotoxicity, dendritic-cell trafficking, phagocytosis, regulatory T-cell function, cytokine balance, and antiviral defense.

DOCK8 is not an established AD gene, biomarker, or validated therapeutic target. Most evidence comes from immunodeficiency, allergy, autoimmunity, infection, cancer, and broader neuroinflammatory contexts. Direct AD-specific evidence is limited to preclinical findings: DOCK8 is upregulated in APP/PS1 mouse hippocampus and in Aβ1-42-stimulated microglia, where it promotes inflammatory activation and plaque-associated pathology via ATF3→DOCK8→Cdc42/p38 MAPK and DOCK8→STAT3→NLRP3→NF-κB cascades. DOCK8 knockdown improved behavioral outcomes in this model.

2. Key Insights

Strongest AD Signal: Microglial Activation

In APP/PS1 mice, hippocampal DOCK8 is increased, ATF3-mediated DOCK8 upregulation drives Cdc42/p38 MAPK activation, enhanced microglial migration toward Aβ plaques, greater plaque-associated pathology, and behavioral impairment. DOCK8 knockdown reversed these effects.

Two Key AD-Relevant Cascades

ATF3 → DOCK8 → Cdc42 → p38 MAPK (migration, actin remodeling) and DOCK8 → STAT3 → NLRP3 → NF-κB (inflammasome, cytokine release, iNOS, CD86). Both are supported by Aβ-stimulated BV2 microglial data.

Biological Plausibility in AD

DOCK8's links to Cdc42/Rac, WASP, Arp2/3, p38 MAPK, STAT3, NF-κB, inflammasome signaling, migration, phagocytosis, and immune synapse formation align with mechanisms relevant to microglial plaque response and chronic AD neuroinflammation.

Major Systemic Safety Concern

Human DOCK8 deficiency causes combined immunodeficiency/hyper-IgE syndrome: recurrent viral, bacterial, and fungal infections; severe eczema; food allergy; eosinophilia; autoimmunity; inflammatory bowel disease; bronchiectasis; malignancy risk; and impaired T-, B-, NK-, and dendritic-cell function.

Context-Dependent Role

In AD-like microglial models, DOCK8 reduction appears protective by lowering inflammatory activation and migration. In systemic immunity, DOCK8 restoration is life-saving because deficiency causes severe disease. Therapeutic direction differs by cell type, disease stage, and compartment.

Safer Downstream Alternatives

Modulating p38 MAPK, STAT3, NLRP3 inflammasome, NF-κB, or Cdc42-linked cytoskeletal signaling may capture AD-relevant inflammatory effects while avoiding the profound immune toxicity of full systemic DOCK8 suppression.

3. Actionable Takeaways

  1. Treat DOCK8 as a speculative AD neuroimmune node, not a validated target. Prioritize AD-specific validation before any therapeutic development.
  2. Assess DOCK8 in AD-specific datasets. Measure expression in AD brain, CSF, blood, single-cell microglia datasets, plaque-associated microglia, border-associated macrophages, monocytes, and infiltrating lymphocytes.
  3. Test whether DOCK8 correlates with AD-relevant outcomes. Evaluate amyloid burden, tau pathology, neuroinflammatory markers, microglial activation states, cognitive trajectory, and systemic immune signatures.
  4. Mechanistic studies should focus on microglia. Determine whether DOCK8 controls Aβ-induced migration, plaque clustering, phagocytosis, inflammasome activation, cytokine release, synapse engulfment, neuronal toxicity, and transition into disease-associated microglial states.
  5. Interrogate the two key signaling axes. ATF3→DOCK8→Cdc42→p38 MAPK and DOCK8→STAT3→NLRP3→NF-κB are the most directly AD-relevant cascades described in the supplied evidence.
  6. Do not pursue global DOCK8 inhibition. Human genetics strongly indicate DOCK8 loss causes severe immune compromise. Any AD strategy must emphasize microglia-selective, brain-targeted, partial, reversible, or downstream-pathway modulation.
  7. Clarify whether DOCK8 inhibition impairs beneficial microglial functions. Reduced migration/phagocytosis could lower neuroinflammation and harmful engulfment but might also impair Aβ containment, debris clearance, or tissue repair.
  8. Use disease-stage-specific models. DOCK8 modulation may have different effects during early amyloid accumulation, plaque maturation, chronic inflammation, tau spread, or late neurodegeneration.
Overall Assessment — DOCK8 is a compelling immune/cytoskeletal regulator with plausible but preliminary AD relevance through microglial migration, Aβ-induced inflammatory activation, Cdc42/p38 MAPK signaling, and STAT3/NLRP3/NF-κB signaling. Its therapeutic potential in AD remains speculative and would require microglia-selective targeting plus strong safety controls, because systemic DOCK8 loss is profoundly immunopathogenic. Downstream pathway modulators (p38 MAPK, STAT3, NLRP3, NF-κB) represent safer near-term AD research priorities.
