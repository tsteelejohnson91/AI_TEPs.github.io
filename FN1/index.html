Gene: FN1  |  Fibronectin 1

FN1 & Alzheimer's Disease

Target Enabling Package — AI-Assisted Evidence Summary

1. Overview

FN1 encodes fibronectin 1, an extracellular-matrix and cell-adhesion glycoprotein repeatedly linked to ECM remodeling, fibrosis, vascular/endothelial dysfunction, immune-cell communication, integrin/CD44 signaling, TGF-β/SMAD activity, PI3K-Akt/FAK/Src signaling, oxidative stress, hypoxia, senescence, and wound-healing-like repair programs.

The evidence connecting FN1 to Alzheimer's disease (AD) is mixed in strength. FN1 appears as part of an AD plasma proteomic classifier, as a candidate resilience-associated AD gene, as a top candidate in a miR-146a-5p AD network, and as carrying a protective variant (rs140926439) that may reduce AD risk in APOEε4 carriers by limiting fibronectin accumulation at the blood–brain barrier.

Overall, FN1 is best interpreted as a plausible AD-relevant vascular–ECM–immune remodeling node rather than a validated amyloid-β or tau therapeutic target.

2. Key Insights

Blood–Brain Barrier & Vascular ECM

Increased fibronectin may accumulate at the BBB in AD. The rs140926439 variant may protect APOEε4 carriers by preventing harmful fibronectin buildup, supporting a vascular-matrix model of AD.

Resilience / Genetic Risk Factor

AD reviews list FN1 among resilience or protective genetic factors alongside APOE3 Christchurch, RELN-COLBOS, TREM2, and PICALM — a framework of preserved cognition rather than direct amyloid/tau targeting.

AD Biomarker Context

A plasma MRM-MS study included FN1 in a 13-protein classifier distinguishing AD from controls (AUC 0.90) and stable vs. progressing MCI (AUC 0.81), as part of a systemic inflammatory–ECM signature.

TGF-β/SMAD-Driven Fibrosis

Across many non-AD models, TGF-β stimulation increases FN1 alongside COL1A1, ACTA2, vimentin, and CTGF. Perivascular fibrosis-like remodeling and impaired clearance are directly relevant to AD neurovascular dysfunction.

Integrin / FAK / PI3K Signaling

FN1 signals through α5β1, αvβ3, ITGB1, CD44, SDC4, and FAK/Src/PI3K-Akt axes. In AD, analogous signaling may influence endothelial cells, pericytes, astrocytes, and microglia.

Inflammatory & Myeloid States

FN1 links to TREM2+ macrophages, SPP1+ niches, FN1+CCR2+ monocyte precursors, and CCL2/CD44/AKT signaling — all central to AD neuroinflammation and border-associated macrophage biology.

3. Actionable Takeaways

  1. Treat as a candidate, not a validated target. Current evidence supports biological plausibility for neurovascular and inflammatory AD endophenotypes, but direct causal AD data remain limited.
  2. Validate FN1 expression and localization in AD tissue. Prioritize AD brain, CSF, plasma vascular fractions, perivascular spaces, plaques, and CAA lesions via single-cell and spatial transcriptomics.
  3. Identify relevant FN1-producing and -responsive cell types. Key candidates: endothelial cells, pericytes, vascular smooth muscle cells, astrocytes, microglia, infiltrating monocytes/macrophages, and perivascular fibroblast-like cells.
  4. Determine whether FN1 is harmful, protective, or stage-dependent. Test whether fibronectin accumulation worsens BBB breakdown, perivascular drainage, Aβ clearance, tau spread, or microglial activation — or whether it supports vascular repair.
  5. Focus on selective pathway modulation, not broad FN1 depletion. Block pathological FN1–integrin or FN1–CD44 signaling; normalize mislocalized fibronectin; target disease-associated isoforms; or suppress upstream TGF-β/SMAD, NF-κB, AGE-RAGE, or FAK/Src.
  6. Explore FN1 in biomarker panels. Plasma, CSF, or extracellular vesicle signatures combining FN1 with ECM remodeling, BBB dysfunction, or vascular inflammatory markers may identify AD patients with MCI progression risk.
  7. Stratify by APOE and vascular-inflammatory context. FN1 biology may be especially relevant in APOEε4 carriers and patients with vascular, BBB, or inflammatory AD features.
  8. Apply safety constraints early. Fibronectin supports wound healing, cell adhesion, vascular stability, and barrier repair. Any FN1-directed therapy must be tested for effects on vascular integrity and normal ECM function to avoid indiscriminate systemic inhibition.
Overall Assessment — FN1 is a plausible but unvalidated AD therapeutic candidate. Its strongest potential relevance is to AD subtypes involving blood–brain barrier dysfunction, cerebral amyloid angiopathy, impaired perivascular clearance, vascular inflammation, glial/perivascular ECM remodeling, systemic inflammation, metabolic vascular disease, or APOEε4-associated vascular vulnerability. It is not currently a primary amyloid-β or tau target.
