HLA-DRA

HLA Class II Histocompatibility Antigen, DR Alpha Chain  |  MHC Class II  |  Plaque Microglial Marker

Neuroimmune Marker Plaque-Associated MHC Class II
Candidate Neuroimmune Biomarker: HLA-DRA shows direct AD brain expression changes, plaque-associated microglial co-expression (with TREM2, TYROBP, APOE), superior temporal gyrus transcriptomic enrichment, and links to tau pathology and cognitive decline. Stronger AD evidence than most candidate immune genes, but not yet a validated therapeutic target.

Overview

HLA-DRA encodes the invariant alpha chain of HLA-DR, the MHC class II heterodimer that presents peptides to CD4+ T cells and coordinates antigen-processing activity across microglia, macrophages, monocytes, dendritic cells, and B cells. Its AD relevance is more direct than most candidate immune genes: HLA-DRA expression is significantly altered in AD brains in immune-mediated disease overlap studies; the HLA-region signal associates with neurofibrillary tangle pathology and longitudinal cognitive decline; and HLA-DRA appears as part of a replicated AD superior temporal gyrus transcriptomic signature enriched for microglial/immune genes (CD74, TREM2, C3AR1, ITGAX, OLR1). Most notably, HLA-DRA is elevated in laser-captured Aβ plaque regions alongside APOE, AXL, TREM2, and TYROBP—especially in early-onset AD plaques—while remaining low in non-plaque parenchyma, suggesting a localized plaque-associated antigen-presenting microglial state. Through the HLA-DRα1/CD74/MIF axis, HLA-DRA also connects to tractable inflammatory signaling. Its most defensible role is as a biomarker and pathway node marking activated plaque-associated microglia, not as a direct drug target; broad MHC class II inhibition could impair phagocytosis, host defense, and potentially beneficial microglial clearance functions.

Key Insights

MHC Class II Antigen Presentation

HLA-DRA encodes the invariant alpha chain of HLA-DR and repeatedly co-appears with CD74, HLA-DRB1/DRB5, HLA-DPA1/DPB1, HLA-DQA1/DQB1, CIITA, HLA-DMA, and HLA-DMB, indicating coordinated antigen-processing and antigen-presentation activity. HLA-DR-positive microglia can present peptides to CD4+ T cells, potentially engaging adaptive immune responses within the CNS. In AD, whether this antigen-presentation activity reflects harmful chronic inflammation or adaptive immune surveillance remains unresolved.

Plaque-Associated Microglia

A key AD-specific finding places HLA-DRA at the center of plaque-associated microglial biology: laser-captured AD plaque regions showed elevated HLA-DRA expression alongside APOE, AXL, TREM2, and TYROBP—particularly in early-onset AD plaques—while non-plaque parenchyma showed low expression. This supports HLA-DRA as marking a spatially localized plaque-response, phagocytic, and antigen-presenting microglial state rather than uniform global brain inflammation. The co-expression with TREM2, TYROBP, C3AR1, and ITGAX places HLA-DRA within disease-associated microglial programs.

Tau Pathology & Cognitive Decline

HLA-DRA was significantly altered in AD brains compared with controls in an immune-mediated disease overlap study; the HLA-region signal associated with neurofibrillary tangle (NFT) burden and longitudinal cognitive decline. HLA-DRA also appeared as part of a replicated AD superior temporal gyrus (STG) transcriptomic signature enriched for microglial/immune genes (CD74, TREM2, C3AR1, ITGAX, OLR1). An NLRP3 inflammasome postmortem study found no significant HLA-DRA difference in temporal cortex, highlighting heterogeneity across brain regions and methods.

Innate-Adaptive Immune Bridge

Because HLA-DR presents peptides to CD4+ T cells, HLA-DRA-positive microglia/macrophages could engage antigen-specific adaptive immune responses at plaques, CNS borders, or perivascular niches. The HLA-DRα1/CD74/MIF axis is mechanistically tractable: non-AD EAE studies show HLA-DRα1-derived constructs can downregulate CD74/MIF inflammatory signaling, reduce NLRP3 and IL-1β, increase M2-like macrophages, and improve CNS inflammatory disease phenotypes. This axis has not been tested in AD models but is mechanistically attractive.

Genetic Architecture & Cross-Trait Overlap

HLA-DRA or the HLA-DRA/HLA-DRB5 region appears in AD CNV, eQTL/SMR, cross-trait, and dementia-risk analyses, as well as in shared genetic architecture with immune-mediated diseases, lipid traits, cardiovascular risk, gastrointestinal disease, and sleep-related traits. The extensive HLA-region linkage disequilibrium means HLA-DRA signals may tag broader haplotypes rather than proving HLA-DRA-specific causality; high-resolution HLA analysis with phasing is required.

Neuroinflammatory State Regulation

HLA-DRA expression is driven by IFN-γ/STAT1/CIITA, NF-κB/TNF, and complement-linked immune-remodeling pathways. In AD, HLA-DRA-positive microglia could be harmful if they amplify chronic neuroinflammation, but potentially beneficial if they support debris handling, plaque containment, or complement-mediated clearance. State-dependent context is critical: low HLA-DRA in sepsis or immunoparalysis marks impaired immune competence, while high HLA-DRA in activated microglia marks antigen-presenting capacity that may be either harmful or protective depending on AD stage and microenvironment.

Actionable Takeaways

  • Treat HLA-DRA as a candidate neuroimmune biomarker, not a validated drug target. Its most defensible role is marking plaque-associated microglial MHC class II activation, correlating with tau pathology and cognitive decline, and defining immune-active AD subtypes.
  • Prioritize cell-type-specific spatial validation. Determine whether HLA-DRA is expressed by plaque-associated microglia, border-associated macrophages, infiltrating monocytes, dendritic-like cells, astrocytes, endothelial/perivascular cells, or choroid plexus cells. Plaque co-localization studies combining spatial transcriptomics with amyloid/tau imaging are critical.
  • Measure HLA-DRA as part of a companion panel. Useful co-markers include CD74, TREM2, TYROBP, APOE, AXL, C3AR1, ITGAX, OLR1, HLA-DRB1/DRB5, CIITA, IRF8, complement genes, interferon-response genes, IL-1β, TNF, and standard microglial activation markers.
  • Explore the HLA-DRα1/CD74/MIF axis as an intervention point. This is the most mechanistically developed therapeutic angle from the evidence. Test whether HLA-DRα1-derived peptides or CD74/MIF inhibitors reduce NLRP3, IL-1β, and plaque-associated neuroinflammation in AD models.
  • Avoid assuming lower HLA-DRA is always beneficial. HLA-DRA-positive plaque microglia co-express TREM2, TYROBP, APOE, and AXL—phagocytic and plaque-clearance genes. Indiscriminate MHC class II suppression could impair protective debris handling or plaque containment.
  • Use HLA-DRA for AD immune-subtype stratification. It may help identify immune-active AD patients with strong microglial antigen-presentation signatures, APOE ε4-linked immune-expression patterns, vascular/lipid comorbidities, or systemic inflammatory burden who may respond differently to immunomodulatory therapies.
  • Require AD-specific causal perturbation data before target nomination. Missing: knockdown/rescue effects on amyloid/tau pathology, synaptic integrity, neurovascular dysfunction, and cognition; safety data on systemic immune consequences of HLA-DRA manipulation; and demonstration that modulating HLA-DRA changes disease trajectory rather than only reflecting immune state.

TEP Verdict

HLA-DRA is one of the more directly AD-relevant immune genes in this series, supported by plaque microglial co-expression, AD brain transcriptomic enrichment, and tau/cognition associations. Its near-term value is as a neuroimmune biomarker for plaque-associated microglial activation and as a patient-stratification tool for immune-active AD subtypes. The HLA-DRα1/CD74/MIF axis represents the most mechanistically tractable therapeutic angle. Broad HLA-DRA inhibition should be avoided due to phagocytic and host-defense consequences. Before advancing to therapeutic target nomination, AD-specific causal perturbation studies—testing whether HLA-DRA modulation alters amyloid burden, tau pathology, synaptic integrity, and cognition—are required.
