NDUFA10

NADH:Ubiquinone Oxidoreductase Subunit A10  |  Complex I Accessory Subunit  |  Mitochondrial Energy Node

Candidate Pathway Complex I Subunit Mitochondrial
Candidate Mitochondrial Pathway Target: NDUFA10 shows mechanistically plausible AD links—cognitive decline correlation, hippocampal OXPHOS network membership, and treatment-responsive expression—but direct causal AD evidence is limited. AD-focused biochemical and interventional validation is needed.

Overview

NDUFA10 is a nuclear-encoded accessory subunit of mitochondrial Complex I (NADH:ubiquinone oxidoreductase), linked to oxidative phosphorylation, ATP production, NADH/ubiquinone electron transfer, redox balance, mitochondrial membrane potential, and intrinsic apoptosis. Direct AD evidence includes: lower NDUFA10 expression correlated with worse MMSE scores; a proposed METTL3–m6A–NDUFA10 regulatory axis in AD risk; hippocampal transcriptomic placement within shared OXPHOS/AD/PD/HD pathway networks; and treatment-associated modulation in tetramethylpyrazine-treated AD mouse models. A mechanistically important finding from PINK1/Parkin biology links NDUFA10 serine-250 phosphorylation to ubiquinone reduction, membrane potential maintenance, ATP synthesis, and synaptic function—with phosphomimetic rescue in PINK1-deficient models. Astrocytic dopamine D2 receptor signaling also requires NDUFA10 for neuroprotective metabolic coupling. NDUFA10 is best treated as a mitochondrial-protective pathway node and biomarker candidate rather than a validated standalone drug target; brain-targeted modulation is preferable given oncologic risks of systemic Complex I enhancement.

Key Insights

Complex I Core Function

NDUFA10 is a nuclear-encoded accessory subunit essential for stable Complex I assembly, NADH/ubiquinone electron transfer, proton pumping, ATP production, NAD+/NADH balance, and mitochondrial membrane potential maintenance. In aged mouse medial prefrontal cortex, NDUFA10 downregulation reduced ATP during metabolic stress; knockdown worsened burst suppression under sevoflurane, while ATP supplementation attenuated it—suggesting NDUFA10-dependent energy reserve protects aging brain circuits.

Cognitive Decline & AD Expression

Direct AD-related evidence includes: lower NDUFA10 expression correlated with worse MMSE scores (more severe dementia); hippocampal transcriptomic analysis placing NDUFA10 among high-degree genes shared across oxidative phosphorylation, AD, PD, and HD pathways; and treatment-associated modulation by tetramethylpyrazine in AD mouse models alongside reduced Aβ and tau phosphorylation. NDUFA10 mutations causing Complex I deficiency and Leigh syndrome confirm the gene's importance in metabolically vulnerable neural tissues.

PINK1/Parkin Phosphorylation Regulation

PINK1/Parkin studies identify NDUFA10 as a regulated Complex I node: loss of NdufA10 serine-250 phosphorylation impaired ubiquinone reduction, membrane potential, ATP synthesis, and synaptic function in PINK1-deficient models. Phosphomimetic NdufA10 rescued these defects, demonstrating that post-translational regulation of NDUFA10 tunes Complex I activity independently of mitophagy. Whether AD alters NDUFA10 phosphorylation at analogous sites is an open and therapeutically relevant question.

ROS & Oxidative Stress Cascade

Reduced or dysfunctional NDUFA10 contributes to a feed-forward oxidative injury cycle: impaired Complex I activity increases superoxide generation, NAD+/NADH imbalance, mitochondrial membrane potential loss, and mPTP opening—driving intrinsic apoptosis. These outcomes align closely with established AD-associated neuronal energy failure and oxidative damage. Interventions that preserved NDUFA10 in ischemia/reperfusion, kidney injury, and cardiomyopathy models consistently improved Complex I activity and ATP production.

Astrocytic & Glial Mechanisms

Astrocytic dopamine D2 receptor (Drd2) signaling preserved mitochondrial function through a β-arrestin2 pathway involving Complex I subunits including NDUFA10; knockdown of astrocytic NDUFA10 abolished the neuroprotective effect. This is directly relevant to AD because impaired astrocyte-neuron metabolic coupling is central to progression. Glial-specific validation of NDUFA10 biology is therefore a priority for AD therapeutic development.

m6A / METTL3 Transcriptional Regulation

A computational AD risk model identified a METTL3–m6A–NDUFA10 regulatory relationship in which low METTL3 combined with low NDUFA10 predicted greater AD likelihood. This suggests upstream RNA-modification control of NDUFA10 expression may be epigenetically dysregulated in AD. Validating whether METTL3-dependent m6A modification directly stabilizes NDUFA10 transcripts in AD-relevant neurons and astrocytes is an actionable next step for target validation.

Actionable Takeaways

  • Prioritize direct AD validation in relevant systems. Measure NDUFA10 mRNA, protein, phosphorylation, Complex I assembly, and activity in AD patient brain (hippocampus, cortex), amyloid/tau animal models, and AD cellular models before advancing therapeutic development.
  • Test causality, not correlation. Determine whether NDUFA10 knockdown worsens—and restoration improves—AD-relevant phenotypes: synaptic function, neuronal survival, ATP levels, ROS burden, mitochondrial membrane potential, Aβ toxicity, tau phosphorylation, and cognitive performance.
  • Investigate PINK1-analogous phosphorylation in AD. Assess whether AD alters NDUFA10 serine-250 (or equivalent) phosphorylation and whether phosphomimetic or kinase-pathway modulation can restore Complex I function in AD-relevant models.
  • Validate the METTL3–m6A–NDUFA10 axis. Test whether METTL3-dependent m6A modification directly stabilizes NDUFA10 transcripts in AD-relevant neurons and astrocytes, establishing an upstream epigenetic intervention point.
  • Include glial and co-culture models. Astrocytic NDUFA10 is required for Drd2-mediated neuroprotection. Evaluate NDUFA10 in astrocytes and neuron-astrocyte co-cultures to capture the full scope of metabolic coupling mechanisms.
  • Use NDUFA10 as part of a mitochondrial biomarker panel, not in isolation. Pair with NDUFA9, NDUFV1, NDUFV2, NDUFS3, NDUFB3, SDHB, COX5B, UQCRFS1, and ATP5A to characterize Complex I integrity holistically.
  • Pursue brain-targeted or cell-type-specific modulation. Systemic NDUFA10/Complex I enhancement poses oncologic and immune-metabolic risks. Any therapeutic approach should favor CNS-targeted delivery or cell-type-restricted modulation strategies.
  • Do not advance NDUFA10 as a standalone confirmed AD drug target at this stage. The evidence is mechanistically strong but largely indirect, computational, or treatment-associated. AD-focused biochemical and interventional studies are the critical next step.

TEP Verdict

NDUFA10 is a biologically plausible mitochondrial pathway node for AD, not yet a validated standalone therapeutic target. The most actionable near-term priorities are: (1) direct AD causal validation—knockdown/rescue experiments in AD-relevant neurons and astrocytes; (2) post-translational characterization of NDUFA10 phosphorylation in AD brain; (3) METTL3–m6A–NDUFA10 axis validation as an upstream epigenetic intervention point; and (4) inclusion of NDUFA10 in multi-subunit OXPHOS biomarker panels for mitochondrial integrity monitoring. If restoration of NDUFA10 function improves AD phenotypes, brain-targeted modulation of Complex I assembly, upstream kinase pathways (PINK1-related), or m6A RNA regulation would be the most mechanistically supported therapeutic routes.
